Maison Farruch

Embroidery’s ancestral home, tuned to the cadence of couture capitals.

Born on the looms of Gujarat and matured in Mumbai, Farruch threads generational savoir-faire into contemporary couture dialogues across Paris, Milan, London, and New York.

The maison and its lineage

Farruch’s story began with the Zariwala family safeguarding India’s rarest embroidery disciplines while the world looked to Paris for couture. Each successive generation vowed to keep the craft intimate, precise, and emotionally resonant.

Today the maison orchestrates every commission from Farruch Tower in Mumbai, then echoes it through liaison salons in Europe and the United States. Collaborations are treated as living manuscripts—researched, edited, and archived for future inspiration.

Heritage

Custodians of the craft

Zafer Zariwala

Fourth-generation embroiderer Zafer Zariwala relocated the family atelier to Mumbai in 1969, marrying Indian needlework with a cosmopolitan sensibility that captured the attention of haute couture houses.

Rizvi Zariwala

As fifth-generation steward, Rizvi founded Farruch & Embroideries Pvt Ltd to formalise the maison’s promise. He mentors artisans personally, translating couture briefs into embroidery narratives with architectural precision.

Farruch Atelier Successors

The sixth generation apprentices alongside master artisans, introducing contemporary proportion and digital fluency while honouring the muscle memory encoded in the maison’s archive.

Riaz Sheikh

As Head of Embroideries, Riaz Sheikh choreographs the maison’s floors—from concept salons to quality control suites—ensuring each piece resonates with Farruch’s lightness and authority.

Atelier Infrastructure

Farruch Tower, Mumbai

Our privately owned ten-storey tower houses every discipline of couture embroidery. Dedicated floors are appointed for hand techniques, machine innovation, tailoring, accessories, warehousing, and the maison’s temperature-controlled archive.

State-of-the-art safety systems, private presentation suites, and discreet logistics corridors allow designers to move from prototype to final ensemble without leaving the building—or compromising confidentiality.
